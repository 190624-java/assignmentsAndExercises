Agile

Agile is a system for software development. Agile enforces the idea of people and how they work together. It stresses adaptive planning, evolutionary development, and rapid and flexible response to change. The emphasis on problem solving through collaboration is key to Agile. Key points to Agile development include daily meetings, incremental and iterative development, and user stories.

Scrum

Scrum is empirical in that it provides a means for teams to establish a hypothesis of how they think something works, try it out, reflect on the experience, and make the appropriate adjustments. Scrum values are commitment, courage, focus, openness, and respect. Its principles are transparency, inspection, and adaptation. Scrum works best that revolve around high workloads that can be broken down into multiple several week blocks of development.

Product Owner

The stakeholder of the project, and the person providing the vision or goal for the project

Project Backlog

Think of this like the list or bullet points of the things to do for the project, on a global scope.

Sprint Planning

This is the planning phase where the product owner proposes a goal, the team members forecast their ability to meet requirements, and the scrum master acts as a liasion to help communicate between the team and the product owner and that the goal is effectively communicated, and that the discussion is productive

Sprint

In it's most basic definition, the sprint is the interim or time-period that the project is determined to last for the features to be implemented.

Standup

Brief meetings that should happen daily to keep communication throughout the team and prevent scope creep, or losing sight of goals and deadlines.

Scrum Master

Essentially a middle-management role designed to keep the team productive. Is not an actual member of the team and has no stake in the project. The entire goal of a scrum master is to keep project cohesion and keep team members productive. More of a coaching/mentoring role than a managerial or supervisory one.

Deliverable

Can be used to describe the final object that development was making. The deliverable should be in a shippable state by the time of the last sprint when the sprint backlog has no more features remaining.

Sprint Backlog

Is the collection of product backlogs for delivery in the sprint. The team then identifies which tasks are necessary in order for the Sprint goal and delivery.

Story Pointing

Method of establishing a baseline for priority within the scope of a sprint. Team members take a specific user story they can make a consensus on, and use that as a baseline for relative diffulty in comparison to this user story, and assign it a number to help visualize complexity, difficulty, and necessity.

    Criteria During Story Pointing

  • Amount of work
  • Complexity of the work
  • Risk or uncertainty of the work
  • Estimated time or duration
